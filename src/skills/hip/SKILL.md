# Hip Clinical Skill

> Body-region clinical reasoning for Vero AI Physical Therapy hip module.

## Region Overview

### Anatomy

The hip is a ball-and-socket synovial joint formed by the femoral head articulating with the acetabulum of the pelvis. Key structures:

- **Osseous:** Femoral head, acetabulum, greater and lesser trochanters. The acetabulum faces anterolaterally with ~15-20 deg anteversion and ~45 deg inclination.
- **Labrum:** Fibrocartilaginous ring deepening the acetabulum by 22%, providing suction seal, load distribution, and proprioception. Most vascularized at periphery; central tears heal poorly.
- **Capsule and ligaments:** Iliofemoral (Y ligament of Bigelow, strongest in body), pubofemoral, ischiofemoral. Capsule tightens in extension and external rotation.
- **Hip flexors:** Iliopsoas (primary), rectus femoris, sartorius, TFL. Iliopsoas inserts on lesser trochanter.
- **Gluteals:** Gluteus maximus (extension, ER), gluteus medius (abduction, pelvic stability), gluteus minimus (abduction, IR). Medius and minimus insert on greater trochanter.
- **Deep external rotators:** Piriformis, obturator internus/externus, gemelli, quadratus femoris. Piriformis exits through greater sciatic notch; sciatic nerve passes deep to (or through) it in ~17% of population.
- **IT band / TFL:** TFL originates at ASIS, merges with IT band, inserts at Gerdy's tubercle on lateral tibia. Functions as lateral hip and thigh stabilizer.
- **Neurovascular:** Sciatic nerve (L4-S3), femoral nerve (L2-L4), obturator nerve (L2-L4), lateral femoral cutaneous nerve, medial circumflex femoral artery (critical supply to femoral head).

### Common Pathologies

| Pathology | Prevalence | Primary Population |
|---|---|---|
| FAI (cam/pincer/mixed) | 10-15% general, 55-75% in hip pain patients | Young active adults, athletes |
| Labral tear | 22-55% of hip/groin pain presentations | Athletes, FAI patients |
| Hip OA | 10-25% adults >55 years | Middle-aged to elderly |
| Greater trochanteric pain syndrome (GTPS) | 10-25% of primary care hip complaints | Women 40-60, runners |
| Gluteal tendinopathy | Most common cause of lateral hip pain | Women 40-60, runners |
| Piriformis syndrome | 6-8% of low back/sciatica presentations | Runners, prolonged sitters |
| Trochanteric bursitis | Component of GTPS | Runners, women |
| Snapping hip (coxa saltans) | 5-10% of dancers, runners | Athletes, dancers |

## Special Tests (Pose-Based)

### 1. FADIR Test (Flexion-Adduction-Internal Rotation)

**Target pathology:** Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI), labral tear.
**Setup:** Patient supine, hip and knee flexed to 90 degrees.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to lie on back, pull knee to chest to 90 deg hip flexion, then bring knee across body (adduction) and rotate foot outward (internal rotation of hip).
**Keypoint validation:** Confirm hip_flexion ~90 deg via landmarks 23-25 (hip-knee). Track adduction angle (knee crossing midline) using landmark 25 relative to ipsilateral landmark 23. Internal rotation measured by ankle (landmark 27) position relative to knee (landmark 25) in transverse plane.
**Positive finding:** Sharp, pinching anterior groin pain (C-sign: patient cups hand around anterior hip). Pain reproduced at ~90 deg flexion + 15 deg adduction + 15 deg IR.
**Interpretation:** Anterior labral impingement or FAI. Sensitivity 96%, Specificity 17%. Excellent screening test (high sensitivity) but poor specificity; a negative FADIR largely rules out intra-articular pathology.
**Angle thresholds:** Hip flexion 85-95 deg, adduction 10-20 deg past midline, internal rotation 10-20 deg.

### 2. FABER Test (Patrick's Test)

**Target pathology:** Hip joint pathology vs. SI joint differentiation.
**Setup:** Patient supine.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to lie on back, place ankle of test leg on opposite knee (figure-4 position), then let test knee fall outward toward the surface.
**Keypoint validation:** Confirm figure-4 position via landmarks. Measure distance from test knee (landmark 25) to floor/surface. Compare sides. Measure hip abduction + external rotation angle using landmarks 23-25-27.
**Positive finding:**
- **Groin pain** -> Hip joint pathology (OA, labral tear, FAI)
- **Posterior/SI pain** -> SI joint dysfunction
- **Asymmetric ROM** (test knee significantly higher than opposite side, >4 cm difference) -> Hip capsular restriction
**Interpretation:** Differentiates hip from SI pathology based on pain location. Also assesses hip ER/ABD flexibility. Sensitivity 57-60%, Specificity 71% for hip OA.

### 3. Trendelenburg Test (Hip Abductor Strength)

**Target pathology:** Gluteus medius weakness/tendinopathy.
**Setup:** Patient standing, facing away from camera or in frontal plane view.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to stand on one leg for 30 seconds. Observe pelvis from behind (or via frontal plane keypoints).
**Keypoint validation:** Track bilateral hip landmark heights (landmarks 23 and 24) during single-leg stance. Calculate pelvic obliquity angle. Measure trunk lateral lean via landmarks 11-12 (shoulders) relative to 23-24 (hips).
**Positive finding:**
- **Positive (uncompensated):** Contralateral pelvis drops > 5 deg within 30 seconds
- **Compensated:** Trunk leans over stance leg to shift center of gravity (more severe weakness)
- **Delayed positive:** Pelvis level initially but drops after 15-20 seconds (endurance deficit)
**Interpretation:** Gluteus medius weakness or tendinopathy on stance leg. Sensitivity 73%, Specificity 77% for gluteus medius pathology.
**Angle thresholds:** Pelvic drop > 5 deg = positive. Trunk lean > 10 deg = compensated positive.

### 4. Thomas Test (Hip Flexor Tightness)

**Target pathology:** Iliopsoas, rectus femoris, TFL/IT band tightness.
**Setup:** Patient supine at edge of bed/table, both knees pulled to chest, then holding one knee while lowering the other.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to lie on back at edge of bed, pull both knees to chest to flatten lumbar spine, hold one knee to chest, let other leg hang off edge.
**Keypoint validation:** Measure hanging leg's hip angle via landmarks 11-23-25 (trunk-hip-knee). Thigh position relative to horizontal. Also measure knee flexion angle via landmarks 23-25-27.
**Positive finding:**
- **Thigh rises above horizontal** (hip flexion > 0 deg) -> Iliopsoas tightness
- **Knee extends** (loss of knee flexion below 80 deg) -> Rectus femoris tightness
- **Thigh abducts** (drifts laterally) -> IT band / TFL tightness
**Interpretation:** Identifies specific hip flexor component restriction. Common contributor to anterior hip pain, lumbar hyperlordosis, and altered gait mechanics.
**Angle thresholds:** Normal = thigh rests at or below horizontal (0 deg hip flexion), knee flexion 80-90 deg. Positive = hip flexion > 5 deg above horizontal.

### 5. Ober's Test (IT Band/TFL Tightness)

**Target pathology:** IT band and TFL tightness/restriction.
**Setup:** Patient sidelying, test leg on top, bottom leg flexed for stability.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to lie on unaffected side, bend bottom knee for stability. Examiner (or self-test): extend top hip ~10 deg, abduct ~25 deg, then release and let the leg drop toward the surface.
**Keypoint validation:** Track adduction angle of top leg via landmarks 23-25 (hip-knee) in frontal plane. Measure thigh angle relative to horizontal.
**Positive finding:** Test leg stays abducted, cannot adduct past neutral (0 deg). Leg remains above horizontal.
**Interpretation:** IT band or TFL tightness. Common in runners, lateral hip pain, patellofemoral dysfunction, and GTPS.
**Angle thresholds:** Normal = leg drops to or below horizontal (>0 deg adduction). Positive = leg remains >10 deg above horizontal.

### 6. Craig's Test (Femoral Anteversion)

**Target pathology:** Excessive femoral anteversion or retroversion.
**Setup:** Patient prone, knee flexed to 90 degrees.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to lie face down, bend knee to 90 deg. Rotate lower leg inward (hip ER) and outward (hip IR) until greater trochanter is most prominent laterally (palpated or estimated).
**Keypoint validation:** Measure tibial angle from vertical (landmarks 25-27, knee-ankle) when trochanter is most lateral. This angle approximates femoral anteversion.
**Positive finding:** Anteversion > 15 deg (normal 8-15 deg in adults). Excessive anteversion associated with in-toeing, anterior hip pain, predisposition to FAI symptoms.
**Interpretation:** Structural finding influencing exercise prescription and FAI management. Not modifiable but informs exercise positioning and activity guidance.

### 7. Log Roll Test (Intra-articular Pathology)

**Target pathology:** Intra-articular hip irritability (OA, labral tear, AVN, loose body).
**Setup:** Patient supine, leg relaxed in neutral.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to lie on back with legs relaxed. Gently roll the entire leg inward and outward (passive IR and ER through small arc ~10-15 deg).
**Keypoint validation:** Track foot/ankle rotation (landmark 27-29-31) as proxy for hip rotation. Movement amplitude should be small (10-15 deg each direction).
**Positive finding:** Pain with gentle passive rotation. This test stresses only the hip capsule and joint surfaces, not periarticular muscles or tendons.
**Interpretation:** Pain = intra-articular pathology (OA, labral tear, AVN, loose body, synovitis). Most specific test for confirming the hip joint itself is the pain source. Sensitivity 56%, Specificity 86%.

### 8. Resisted Hip Flexion (Stinchfield Test)

**Target pathology:** Labral tear provocation, hip joint irritability.
**Setup:** Patient supine, hip flexed to 30 degrees, knee extended.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to lie on back, raise straight leg to ~30 deg, then hold against resistance (self-resist by pressing down on thigh with hand, or use gravity with ankle weight).
**Keypoint validation:** Confirm hip flexion ~20-30 deg via landmarks 23-25-27. Knee in full extension. Monitor for trunk compensation (flexion, rotation).
**Positive finding:** Deep anterior groin pain reproduced. Pain typically deep and aching, localized to hip joint.
**Interpretation:** Loads acetabular labrum via increased intra-articular pressure from iliopsoas contraction adjacent to labrum. Sensitivity 59%, Specificity 32% for labral pathology. More useful as confirmatory test combined with FADIR.

## Exercise-to-Finding Matrix

| Finding | Phase 1 (Acute / Protected, 0-4 wks) | Phase 2 (Subacute, 4-8 wks) | Phase 3 (Strengthening, 8-14 wks) | Phase 4 (Return to Activity, 14+ wks) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **FAI (cam/pincer)** | AROM within pain-free range (avoid deep flexion + IR), aquatic exercise, isometric glutes, prone hip extension | Hip strengthening avoiding impingement positions (no squat >90 deg), core stability, side-lying hip abduction, clamshells | Progressive loading (box squats, wide stance), single-leg exercises within safe ROM, deadlift variants | Sport-specific drills, full squats if tolerated (avoid end-range impingement), plyometrics within ROM limits |
| **Labral tear (conservative)** | Pain-free AROM, isometric hip strengthening (abductors, extensors), avoid FADIR position, stationary bike (seat height raised) | Progressive hip strengthening, core stability, proprioception (single-leg balance), bridging progressions | Functional loading, single-leg squat, lateral band walks, sport-specific movement patterns | Return to activity with maintained movement control, monitoring for mechanical symptoms |
| **Hip OA** | Gentle AROM all planes, aquatic exercise, stationary bike (low resistance), isometric quadriceps and glutes | Hip strengthening (abductors, extensors, rotators), walking program (progressive duration), sit-to-stand | Progressive resistance training, functional training (stairs, reaching), balance exercises, tai chi | Community exercise program, self-management, activity modification as needed |
| **Gluteal tendinopathy** | Isometric hip abduction (pain-free positions, wall lean), avoid side-lying on affected side, avoid adduction stretch | Isotonic hip abduction (side-lying, standing), hip hikes, modified clamshells, side-lying leg lifts | Heavy slow resistance abduction, single-leg stance progressions, step-ups, functional tasks | Running gait retraining, single-leg functional tasks, load management education |
| **Piriformis syndrome** | Piriformis stretching (FAIR position), neural glides if sciatic component, avoid prolonged sitting, heat | Hip rotator strengthening (prone ER, seated ER), core stability, gluteal activation (bridges), neural mobilization | Functional hip rotation control, progressive loading, running gait analysis, dynamic stretching | Sport-specific hip rotation drills, sitting ergonomics education, maintenance program |
| **Hip flexor strain** | Rest from aggravating activities, gentle AROM flexion (pain-free), isometric hip flexion (submaximal), ice | Progressive hip flexor strengthening (supine marches, standing hip flexion), eccentric loading, hip flexor stretching | Full ROM hip flexor loading, running progression, resisted hip flexion (bands, cable), core integration | Sprinting progression, sport-specific acceleration drills, reactive movements |
| **Trochanteric bursitis** | Avoid side-lying on affected side, isometric glute med (wall lean), ice, activity modification | Side-lying hip abduction (pillow between legs initially), standing hip abduction, clamshells, gait retraining | Progressive resistance hip abductors, single-leg balance, step-ups, lateral band walks | Running gait optimization, load management, maintenance strengthening |

## Angle Windows per Exercise

### Normal Hip ROM Reference Values

| Motion | Normal Range | Functional Minimum | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flexion | 120-135 deg | 90 deg (sit-stand), 120 deg (tying shoes) | <90 deg = functional limitation |
| Extension | 10-30 deg | 10 deg (normal gait) | <10 deg = gait compensation (lumbar lordosis) |
| Abduction | 40-50 deg | 20 deg (gait stability) | <20 deg = Trendelenburg risk |
| Adduction | 20-30 deg | 10 deg (gait crossover) | Excessive = FAI risk position |
| Internal rotation | 30-40 deg | 15 deg | <15 deg = FAI indicator, hip OA marker |
| External rotation | 40-60 deg | 15 deg | Limited = capsular restriction |

### Exercise-Specific Angle Targets

- **Clamshell:** Hip flexion 45 deg, knee flexion 90 deg, abduction/ER 30-45 deg. Do not allow pelvis to roll backward. Target: gluteus medius activation with minimal TFL.
- **Bridge:** Hip extension 0-30 deg, knees flexed 90 deg. Full hip extension at top (hips level with knees and shoulders). Avoid lumbar hyperextension.
- **Side-lying hip abduction:** 0-30 deg abduction range. Slight hip extension (10 deg behind neutral) to bias gluteus medius over TFL. Neutral rotation (avoid ER).
- **Squat (OA/FAI modified):** Hip flexion limit 0-90 deg (avoid >90 deg in FAI). Knees tracking over 2nd toe. Wider stance for FAI patients. Hip hinge emphasis.
- **Single-leg squat:** Hip flexion 0-60 deg, knee flexion 0-60 deg. Watch for: pelvic drop (Trendelenburg), knee valgus, trunk lean. Quality over depth.
- **Hip flexor stretch (half-kneeling):** Trailing hip extended 10-20 deg, posterior pelvic tilt maintained. Avoid lumbar hyperextension during stretch.
- **Prone hip extension:** 0-20 deg extension. Initiate with gluteus maximus, not lumbar extensors. No lumbar lordosis increase.

## Red Flags (IMMEDIATE Session Halt and Referral)

- **Acute hip fracture:** Severe pain after fall + inability to bear weight + shortened/externally rotated leg -> EMERGENCY referral. Most common in elderly with osteoporosis.
- **Avascular necrosis (AVN):** Insidious groin pain + risk factors (corticosteroid use, alcohol abuse >400ml/week, sickle cell disease, radiation, organ transplant, SLE) -> URGENT referral for MRI. Early detection critical for femoral head preservation.
- **Septic arthritis:** Hot, swollen, erythematous hip joint + fever (>38.5C) + severe pain with any ROM + inability to bear weight -> EMERGENCY referral. Delay = joint destruction.
- **Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE):** Adolescent (10-16, often obese) + groin/knee pain + antalgic gait + obligate external rotation with hip flexion + limited IR -> EMERGENCY referral. Non-weight-bearing until surgical fixation.
- **Femoral neck stress fracture:** Insidious groin pain in runner/athlete + pain with hopping on affected leg + risk factors (female athlete triad, relative energy deficiency, rapid training volume increase >10%/week, amenorrhea) -> URGENT referral, immediate non-weight-bearing. Completion risk if compression-side fracture missed.
- **Legg-Calve-Perthes disease:** Child 4-10 years + limp + groin/knee referred pain + limited hip IR and abduction -> URGENT referral for imaging.
- **Progressive joint destruction:** Rapid worsening over days to weeks -> Rapidly destructive OA, infection, or neoplasm. Refer for imaging.
- **Night pain unrelated to position:** Constant, unremitting, wakes from sleep -> Screen for tumor, infection, inflammatory arthritis.
- **Referred cardiac/vascular pain:** Iliac artery endofibrosis (competitive cyclist with exertional thigh claudication), aortic aneurysm (elderly + pulsatile abdominal mass) -> URGENT vascular referral.
- **Cauda equina syndrome signs:** Bilateral leg weakness, saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction -> EMERGENCY referral. Not a hip condition but may present with bilateral hip/groin symptoms.
- **Femoral nerve palsy:** Acute quadriceps weakness + anterior thigh numbness without traumatic cause -> URGENT neurology referral.
- **Inflammatory arthritis features:** Bilateral symmetric joint involvement, prolonged morning stiffness (>60 min), elevated inflammatory markers, age <40 -> Refer rheumatology.

## Progression / Regression Criteria

### Progression Criteria

Advance to next phase when ALL met for 3 consecutive sessions:
1. Pain during exercise <= 3/10 (not increasing post-session or lasting >2 hours)
2. No catching, locking, or giving way during exercises
3. Trendelenburg test negative or improving (pelvic drop < 5 deg for 30 seconds)
4. Single-leg squat quality: no compensatory pelvic drop, trunk lean, or dynamic valgus
5. Form quality >= 80% across all prescribed exercises
6. RPE < 5/10 at current load
7. Strength symmetry: affected/unaffected limb ratio > 70% (phase 1-2), > 80% (phase 2-3), > 90% (phase 3-4)
8. LEFS score stable or improving

### Regression Criteria

Regress to previous phase when ANY occur:
1. Pain during exercise > 5/10
2. Pain increase lasting > 2 hours post-session
3. New or increased mechanical symptoms (catching, locking, clicking with pain)
4. Increased night pain or rest pain (not position-dependent)
5. Compensated Trendelenburg pattern despite verbal and visual cueing
6. Form quality < 50% consistently (significant pelvic and trunk compensations)
7. LEFS worsening >= 9 points (exceeds MCID, indicates functional decline)
8. New neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness in dermatomal pattern)

## Clinical Pearls

- **Hip-spine syndrome:** Hip OA and lumbar spinal stenosis frequently coexist. Reduced hip ROM (especially extension and IR) increases lumbar spine compensation demands. Always screen the lumbar spine in hip patients and vice versa. If both present, address the more symptomatic region first but include exercises for both.
- **Gluteus medius = the hip's rotator cuff:** Tendinopathy and tears of gluteus medius are underdiagnosed, often mislabeled as "bursitis." MRI prevalence of gluteal tendinopathy in lateral hip pain is 50-70%. Treatment is progressive loading (isometric -> isotonic -> heavy slow resistance), NOT stretching. Compressive loads (adduction across midline, side-lying on affected side) aggravate the condition.
- **Avoid stretching GTPS:** Traditional IT band stretching over the greater trochanter compresses the bursa and irritated tendons against the trochanter. Focus on strengthening and load management. Adduction stretches are contraindicated in acute gluteal tendinopathy.
- **FAI screening in young athletes:** Any young athlete (<35) with groin pain and limited hip IR (<15 deg) should be screened for FAI. Cam morphology is present in up to 75% of male athletes with groin pain. Exercise modifications: avoid deep squat (>90 deg hip flexion), especially with IR + adduction. Use box squats, limit depth, wider stance, toe-out.
- **Hip OA exercise dosage:** Moderate exercise does NOT accelerate OA progression. Strong evidence supports exercise as first-line treatment. Optimal dose: 2-3 sessions/week, 30-60 minutes, moderate intensity. Reassure patients that joint loading is beneficial. Aquatic exercise is equally effective and preferred when land-based exercise is too painful.
- **Gluteal tendinopathy loading protocol:** Follow tendinopathy loading principles. Start isometric (45-second holds, 5 reps, 3x/day, pain <=4/10 during). Progress to isotonic when isometric pain-free. Heavy slow resistance (8-12 RM, 3 sets, every 48 hours) as final loading stage. Allow 12 weeks minimum for tendon adaptation.
- **Piriformis vs. lumbar disc:** Key differentiators: piriformis syndrome has local buttock tenderness, pain with FAIR test (flexion-adduction-IR), negative straight leg raise (or positive only with hip in flexion-adduction), negative lumbar exam. Lumbar disc has dermatomal pattern, positive SLR at 30-60 deg, possible motor/sensory deficit, positive lumbar provocation.
- **Bilateral comparison:** Always compare hip ROM, strength, and Trendelenburg bilaterally. Asymmetry >15% is clinically significant. IR asymmetry >10 deg suggests structural or capsular difference.
- **Gait analysis integration:** Single most informative functional assessment for hip patients. Key deviations: Trendelenburg gait (pelvic drop), antalgic pattern (shortened stance phase), compensated Trendelenburg (trunk lean), toe-out gait (avoiding IR), shortened stride length.
- **Internal rotation is the sentinel motion:** Loss of IR is often the first ROM deficit in FAI, OA, and labral pathology. Monitor IR closely as a treatment response indicator. Normal asymmetry is <5 deg.

## Outcome Measures

| Measure | Full Name | Domains | MCID | MDC | Scoring | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **LEFS** | Lower Extremity Functional Scale | 20 items, general lower extremity function | 9 points | 9 points | 0-80 (higher = better) | All hip patients, general functional tracking |
| **Harris Hip Score** | Harris Hip Score (modified) | Pain, function, ROM, deformity | 7-10 points | 6 points | 0-100 (higher = better) | Hip OA, post-surgical, older adults |
| **iHOT-33** | International Hip Outcome Tool | Symptoms, function, sport, quality of life (33 items on VAS) | 6.1 points | 10.8 points | 0-100 (higher = better) | Young active patients, FAI, labral tears, pre/post arthroscopy |
| **HOOS** | Hip Disability and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score | Pain, symptoms, ADL, sport/recreation, QOL (5 subscales) | 8-10 points per subscale | Varies by subscale | 0-100 per subscale (higher = better) | Hip OA (comprehensive), research |
| **NPRS** | Numeric Pain Rating Scale | Pain intensity (0-10) | 2 points | 2 points | 0-10 (lower = better) | Every session, track pain trajectory |
| **Patient-Specific Functional Scale** | PSFS | Patient-identified functional limitations | 2 points per activity | 3 points | 0-10 per activity (higher = better) | Individualized goal tracking, all diagnoses |

**Reassessment schedule:** NPRS every session. LEFS or condition-specific measure (iHOT-33, Harris Hip Score) at baseline, 4 weeks, 8 weeks, 12 weeks, and discharge. Compare to MCID at each interval to determine clinically meaningful change.
