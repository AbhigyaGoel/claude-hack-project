# Ankle Clinical Skill

> Body-region clinical reasoning for Vero AI Physical Therapy ankle module.

## Region Overview

### Anatomy

The ankle complex comprises the talocrural (true ankle) joint, subtalar joint, and distal tibiofibular syndesmosis. Key structures:

- **Osseous:** Distal tibia (medial malleolus, plafond), distal fibula (lateral malleolus), talus (dome, neck, head), calcaneus, navicular. The mortise formed by tibia and fibula constrains the trapezoidal talus.
- **Lateral ligament complex:**
  - ATFL (anterior talofibular ligament): weakest, most commonly injured (70% of sprains). Resists anterior translation and IR in plantarflexion. Taut in plantarflexion.
  - CFL (calcaneofibular ligament): resists inversion in neutral and dorsiflexion. Taut in dorsiflexion. Injured in 20-40% of sprains (combined ATFL+CFL = grade II-III).
  - PTFL (posterior talofibular ligament): strongest lateral ligament, rarely injured in isolation. Resists posterior translation and ER.
- **Deltoid ligament (medial):** Broad, fan-shaped, strongest ankle ligament complex. Superficial (tibionavicular, tibiocalcaneal, tibiotalar) and deep (anterior/posterior tibiotalar) layers. Resists eversion and lateral talar shift. Injury suggests high-energy mechanism.
- **Syndesmosis:** Anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (AITFL), posterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (PITFL), interosseous membrane. Maintains tibiofibular relationship under axial load. Injury = "high ankle sprain," recovery 2-3x longer than lateral sprain.
- **Achilles tendon:** Gastrocnemius + soleus insert via Achilles on posterior calcaneus. Strongest tendon in the body. Watershed zone of relative hypovascularity 2-6 cm proximal to insertion (common rupture and tendinopathy site). Withstands forces up to 12x body weight during running.
- **Peroneal tendons:** Peroneus longus and brevis course posterior to lateral malleolus in a shared fibro-osseous tunnel. Dynamic lateral ankle stabilizers. Subluxation/dislocation with torn superior peroneal retinaculum.
- **Posterior tibialis tendon:** Courses posterior to medial malleolus, inserts on navicular and cuneiforms. Primary dynamic arch support. Dysfunction leads to adult-acquired flatfoot.
- **Plantar fascia:** Thick connective tissue from medial calcaneal tubercle to metatarsal heads via plantar aponeurosis. Windlass mechanism: toe extension tightens fascia, elevates arch, stabilizes midfoot for push-off.
- **Neurovascular:** Tibial nerve (tarsal tunnel), deep peroneal nerve (anterior), superficial peroneal nerve (lateral), sural nerve (posterior-lateral). Posterior tibial artery, dorsalis pedis artery.

### Common Pathologies

| Pathology | Prevalence | Primary Population |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral ankle sprain | ~2 million/year (US), most common sports injury | Athletes, all ages |
| Chronic ankle instability (CAI) | 20-40% of lateral sprains develop CAI | Post-sprain, young athletes |
| Achilles midportion tendinopathy | 6-17% of runners | Runners 35-55, recreational athletes |
| Achilles insertional tendinopathy | 25% of Achilles tendinopathies | Middle-aged, less active individuals |
| Plantar fasciitis | 10% lifetime incidence, 1 million visits/year (US) | Runners, age 40-60, BMI >30 |
| High ankle sprain (syndesmosis) | 1-18% of ankle sprains (higher in contact sports) | Contact sport athletes |
| Ankle stress fracture | 10% of all stress fractures | Runners, military, female athletes |

| Peroneal tendon pathology | Often misdiagnosed as chronic sprain | Athletes with recurrent sprains |

## Special Tests (Pose-Based)

### 1. Anterior Drawer Test (ATFL Laxity)

**Target pathology:** ATFL integrity, anterior ankle instability.
**Setup:** Patient seated, ankle in 20 deg plantarflexion (relaxes ATFL for maximal excursion), knee flexed 90 deg. Stabilize distal tibia, apply anterior force to calcaneus/talus.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to sit with foot off edge of chair in slight plantarflexion. Self-test: stabilize shin with one hand, cup heel with other hand and pull foot forward. Observe anterior translation and dimple sign (sulcus at anterolateral ankle).
**Keypoint validation:** Measure anterior tibiotalar displacement relative to baseline position. Track ankle landmark (27) displacement relative to knee (25). Compare bilateral.
**Positive finding:** Excessive anterior translation (>3 mm vs. contralateral), soft or absent endpoint, visible dimple/sulcus at anterolateral ankle.
**Interpretation:** ATFL laxity indicating lateral ankle instability. Sensitivity 73%, Specificity 97% (with anesthesia); 58%, 92% (without). Most reliable 4-5 days post-injury after hemarthrosis resolves.
**Angle thresholds:** >3 mm anterior translation or >3 mm asymmetry vs. contralateral.

### 2. Talar Tilt Test (CFL Laxity)

**Target pathology:** CFL integrity, combined lateral ligament instability.
**Setup:** Patient seated, ankle in neutral dorsiflexion (CFL taut in neutral). Stabilize tibia, apply inversion force to calcaneus.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to sit with foot in neutral. Self-test: stabilize lower leg, attempt to invert heel. Assess degree of tilt and endpoint quality.
**Keypoint validation:** Measure inversion angle under stress. Track heel/calcaneus alignment (landmarks 27-29) relative to tibia. Compare bilateral tilt angles.
**Positive finding:** >5 deg talar tilt more than contralateral side, or absolute tilt >15 deg. Soft endpoint.
**Interpretation:** CFL laxity (combined ATFL+CFL injury = grade II-III sprain). Bilateral CFL laxity = generalized ligamentous laxity, not necessarily pathological. Sensitivity 52%, Specificity 88%.
**Angle thresholds:** >10 deg absolute tilt or >5 deg asymmetry = positive.

### 3. Squeeze Test (Syndesmosis Screening)

**Target pathology:** Distal tibiofibular syndesmosis injury (high ankle sprain).
**Setup:** Patient seated or supine, knee extended.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to squeeze lower leg (tibia and fibula together) at mid-calf level, compressing fibula toward tibia.
**Keypoint validation:** Compression applied at mid-tibial level. Pain assessed at distal syndesmosis (anterior ankle, between malleoli). Not directly measurable via pose landmarks; relies on patient pain report and location indication.
**Positive finding:** Pain at the distal tibiofibular syndesmosis (anterior ankle) when compression is applied at mid-calf. Pain must be at the syndesmosis, not at the squeeze site.
**Interpretation:** Syndesmosis injury (high ankle sprain). Sensitivity 30%, Specificity 93%. Low sensitivity means a negative test does not rule out syndesmosis injury; combine with external rotation test.

### 4. External Rotation (Kleiger) Test (Syndesmosis)

**Target pathology:** Syndesmosis injury, deltoid ligament injury.
**Setup:** Patient seated, knee flexed 90 deg, ankle in neutral.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to sit with knee bent 90 deg and foot flat. Stabilize tibia, externally rotate foot/forefoot on the fixed leg. Slow, controlled rotation.
**Keypoint validation:** Track foot external rotation angle via landmarks 27-29-31 (ankle-heel-toe) relative to tibial axis. Measure rotation range and note angle at pain onset.
**Positive finding:** Pain at anterolateral ankle (syndesmosis) and/or medial ankle (deltoid). Lateral pain = syndesmosis. Medial pain = deltoid injury.
**Interpretation:** Syndesmosis sprain. Sensitivity 71%, Specificity 63%. Combined with squeeze test: sensitivity 81% for syndesmosis injury.
**Angle thresholds:** Pain onset with <15 deg external rotation = significant (normal ER ~15-25 deg pain-free).

### 5. Thompson Test (Achilles Rupture)

**Target pathology:** Achilles tendon rupture (EMERGENCY if positive).
**Setup:** Patient prone or kneeling on chair with feet hanging over edge.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to kneel on chair with feet off edge, or lie face down. Squeeze calf muscle firmly with both hands.
**Keypoint validation:** Observe for plantarflexion response at ankle (landmarks 25-27-29). Normal = foot plantarflexes when calf squeezed. Positive = no plantarflexion response.
**Positive finding:** No plantarflexion of the foot when calf is squeezed = Achilles rupture.
**Interpretation:** Complete Achilles tendon rupture requiring URGENT surgical/orthopedic referral. Sensitivity 96%, Specificity 93%. False negative possible with intact plantaris or partial tear. HALT session and refer immediately if positive.

### 6. Weight-Bearing Lunge Test (Dorsiflexion ROM)

**Target pathology:** Dorsiflexion restriction (gastrocnemius/soleus tightness, anterior impingement, post-immobilization stiffness).
**Setup:** Patient standing facing wall, test foot perpendicular to wall. Lunge knee toward wall while keeping heel on ground.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to stand facing wall, place test foot perpendicular to wall with big toe ~10 cm from wall. Lunge knee toward wall keeping heel down. Progressively move foot back until knee can just touch wall with heel on ground.
**Keypoint validation:** Measure ankle dorsiflexion angle via landmarks 25-27-29 (knee-ankle-heel/foot). Also measure knee-to-wall distance from toe tip to wall (landmark 29 to wall reference). Compare bilateral.
**Positive finding:** Knee-to-wall distance <10 cm or dorsiflexion angle <35 deg (combined tibia + ankle) = restricted. Asymmetry >1.5 cm = clinically significant.
**Interpretation:** Dorsiflexion restriction impacts squat mechanics, running gait, landing strategy, and predisposes to ankle sprain recurrence, Achilles tendinopathy, and plantar fasciitis. Excellent reliability (ICC 0.97-0.98).
**Angle thresholds:** Normal knee-to-wall: 10-12 cm. Normal weight-bearing DF: 35-45 deg. Restricted: <10 cm or <35 deg.

### 7. Single-Leg Balance Test (Proprioception)

**Target pathology:** Proprioceptive deficit, chronic ankle instability, vestibular contribution.
**Setup:** Patient standing on one leg. Progressive challenge: eyes open -> eyes closed. 30-second trials.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to stand on affected leg, arms across chest, opposite foot lifted. Time how long balance is maintained (up to 30 seconds). Note strategy: ankle strategy (subtle sway corrections at ankle) vs. hip strategy (large trunk/hip movements) vs. step-off.
**Keypoint validation:** Track center of mass displacement via hip landmarks (23-24) and trunk (11-12). Count step-offs (any ground touch = failure for that trial). Calculate sway area/path length from landmark trajectory.
**Positive finding:**
- Eyes open: unable to maintain >30 seconds = deficit
- Eyes closed: unable to maintain >10 seconds = significant proprioceptive deficit
- Hip strategy dominant = poor ankle proprioception
- >3 step-offs in 30 seconds = impaired postural control
**Interpretation:** Proprioceptive deficit common after lateral ankle sprain (present in 85% of CAI patients). Single greatest risk factor for ankle sprain recurrence. Essential to address in all ankle rehab programs.
**Time thresholds:** Age-adjusted norms: 18-39 years: 30s eyes open, 25s eyes closed. 40-59: 30s open, 10s closed. >60: 25s open, 5s closed.

### 8. Windlass Test (Plantar Fasciitis)

**Target pathology:** Plantar fasciitis, plantar fasciopathy.
**Setup:** Patient seated or standing. Passive dorsiflexion of the great toe.
**Execution via pose:** Cue patient to sit with foot on ground. Passively extend (dorsiflex) the great toe by pulling it upward. Can also perform weight-bearing: stand on a step with toes over edge, lower heel to stretch, then dorsiflex great toe.
**Keypoint validation:** Track great toe position relative to metatarsal head (landmarks 31-29). MTP dorsiflexion angle. Plantar fascia tension reflected by medial arch height change (visible via medial foot profile).
**Positive finding:** Pain at medial calcaneal tubercle (plantar heel) reproduced by passive great toe dorsiflexion. May see arch height increase (windlass mechanism engaging).
**Interpretation:** Plantar fasciitis / fasciopathy. Sensitivity 32%, Specificity 100% (highly specific when positive). Positive Windlass = high confidence plantar fascia involvement. Negative does not rule out; combine with palpation history.
**Angle thresholds:** Great toe dorsiflexion to ~30-40 deg should reproduce symptoms if fascia is involved.

| Finding | Phase 1 (Acute, 0-2 wks) | Phase 2 (Subacute, 2-6 wks) | Phase 3 (Strengthening, 6-12 wks) | Phase 4 (Return to Sport, 12+ wks) |

| **Lateral sprain (Grade III)** | Immobilization/brace 1-2 weeks, protected WB (boot/crutches), isometrics only, edema management | Progressive WB, gentle AROM, aquatic therapy, isometric progressing to isotonic | Same as Grade I-II Phase 3 but delayed timeline, brace for sport until Phase 4 cleared | Same as Grade I-II Phase 4, bracing for 6-12 months during sport, consider surgical referral if persistent instability |

| **Achilles midportion tendinopathy** | Isometric calf raises (bilateral, 45s holds, 5 reps, 3x/day), heel lift in shoe (1cm), avoid walking barefoot, relative rest from running | Alfredson eccentric heel drops (see protocol below), or heavy slow resistance (HSR) calf raises, bilateral -> unilateral progression | Unilateral eccentric/HSR loading, progressive calf raise loading (bodyweight -> weighted), introduce low-impact running on treadmill | Return to full running (gradual volume increase <10%/week), plyometric introduction, sport-specific loading |

## Angle Windows per Exercise

### Normal Ankle ROM Reference Values

| Motion | Normal Range | Functional Minimum | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dorsiflexion | 10-20 deg (non-WB), 35-45 deg (WB lunge) | 10 deg (gait), 15 deg (stairs), 25 deg (running) | <10 deg = compensatory gait, injury risk |
| Plantarflexion | 40-55 deg | 20 deg (push-off) | Reduced = weak push-off, gait deviation |
| Inversion | 30-35 deg | N/A | Excessive = ligamentous laxity |
| Eversion | 15-20 deg | 5 deg (gait) | Limited = subtalar stiffness |

### Exercise-Specific Angle Targets

- **Eccentric heel drop (Alfredson):** Start in full plantarflexion (bilateral raise up), lower affected leg into dorsiflexion 0-15 deg past neutral over step edge. Knee straight (gastrocnemius emphasis) and knee bent 30 deg (soleus emphasis). Controlled 3-second eccentric phase.
- **Single-leg balance:** Ankle in neutral (0 deg DF). Corrections via ankle strategy: <5 deg sway in any direction = good control. Hip strategy (visible trunk/hip movement) = poor ankle control.
- **Weight-bearing lunge test:** Target >10 cm knee-to-wall distance or >35 deg DF angle. Measure with inclinometer on anterior tibia or via pose landmarks 25-27-29.
- **Calf raise (bilateral):** Full ROM: plantarflexion 30-45 deg (heel raise maximum), return to neutral (0 deg). 3-second concentric, 3-second eccentric for HSR protocol.
- **Calf raise (unilateral):** Same ROM as bilateral. Body should remain vertical (no forward lean). Knee straight for gastrocnemius, knee bent 20-30 deg for soleus.
- **Theraband inversion/eversion:** 0-25 deg inversion range, 0-15 deg eversion range. Controlled tempo, 2s concentric, 2s hold, 3s eccentric.
- **Single-leg squat (ankle focus):** Monitor for ankle DF >10 deg, no excessive pronation (medial arch collapse), knee tracking over 2nd toe. Heel must stay on ground throughout.

## Red Flags (IMMEDIATE Session Halt and Referral)

- **Ottawa Ankle Rules met:** Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of either malleolus, OR inability to weight-bear 4 steps immediately and at evaluation -> Refer for X-ray. Sensitivity 98-99.6% for fracture. Most validated clinical decision rule in emergency medicine.
- **Achilles tendon rupture:** Sudden "pop" during push-off activity + positive Thompson test (no plantarflexion with calf squeeze) + palpable gap in tendon + weakness in plantarflexion -> EMERGENCY orthopedic referral. Do not delay; early surgical/conservative management critical. Delay >48 hours worsens outcomes.
- **Compartment syndrome:** Severe pain disproportionate to injury + pain with passive stretch of affected compartment + firm/tense compartment on palpation + paresthesias -> EMERGENCY surgical referral. 6-hour window for fasciotomy. Most common in tibial fractures but also post-crush/high-energy injury.
- **Stress fracture:** Insidious onset localized bone pain + point tenderness over bone + pain with hopping/impact + risk factors (female athlete triad, rapid training increase >10%/week, amenorrhea, low BMD, military recruit) -> Refer for imaging (MRI preferred over X-ray for early detection). Non-weight-bearing until cleared. Medial malleolus and talus stress fractures have higher nonunion risk.
- **DVT (deep vein thrombosis):** Unilateral calf swelling + warmth + tenderness + Homan's sign (pain with passive DF, though low sensitivity) + risk factors (recent immobilization, surgery, oral contraceptives, malignancy, long travel) -> URGENT referral for Doppler ultrasound. Do NOT massage calf.
- **Foot drop (peroneal nerve injury):** Acute inability to dorsiflex foot or evert + steppage gait + sensory loss dorsum of foot + history of fibular head trauma, tight cast, or knee surgery -> URGENT neurology referral.
- **Infection signs:** Hot, red, swollen joint/wound + fever + red streaking (lymphangitis) + recent wound/surgery -> EMERGENCY referral for septic arthritis or cellulitis.
- **Night pain unrelated to position in adolescent:** Constant, worsening, unresponsive to rest -> Tumor screen (osteoid osteoma common in ankle/foot region in young patients).
- **Bilateral Achilles/plantar symptoms + enthesopathy pattern:** In young male with morning stiffness >60 min -> Screen for seronegative spondyloarthropathy (ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis). Refer rheumatology.

## Alfredson Protocol Detail

### Standard Eccentric Heel Drop Program

The Alfredson protocol is the most evidence-based exercise intervention for midportion Achilles tendinopathy (Level I evidence). Key principles:

**Dosage:**
- 3 sets of 15 repetitions, 2x/day (morning and evening) = 180 total reps/day
- 7 days/week (no rest days in original protocol)
- 12-week minimum program duration
- Perform with knee straight (gastrocnemius) AND knee bent 30 deg (soleus) = 6 total sets per session

**Execution:**
1. Stand on edge of step on forefoot (bilateral)
2. Rise up onto toes using both legs (concentric phase with BOTH legs)
3. Transfer weight to affected leg only
4. Slowly lower heel below step level over 3-5 seconds (eccentric phase on AFFECTED leg only)
5. Return to start using both legs (do NOT concentrically load affected leg alone)
6. Repeat with knee straight (3x15), then knee bent 30 deg (3x15)

**Pain Monitoring:**
- Pain during exercise is EXPECTED and ACCEPTABLE up to 5/10
- Pain should not increase progressively during a set
- Pain should return to baseline within 24 hours
- If pain is <3/10 during exercise -> ADD LOAD (backpack with weight, weighted vest, or hold dumbbells)
- If pain is >5/10 consistently -> REDUCE LOAD or reduce to 2x12 reps
- STOP if: pain >7/10, sharp/sudden pain, morning pain worsening week over week

**Progression:**
- Weeks 1-2: bodyweight, bilateral concentric, unilateral eccentric
- Weeks 3-4: add 5 kg if pain <3/10 during exercise
- Weeks 5-8: progressive loading (5 kg increments as tolerated)
- Weeks 9-12: heavy loading, introduce bilateral concentric-eccentric if tolerated
- Week 12+: transition to heavy slow resistance (HSR) maintenance program

**Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) Alternative:**
Recent evidence (Beyer et al. 2015) shows HSR produces equivalent outcomes with better patient satisfaction and compliance:
- Seated and standing calf raises with progressive loading
- 3 sets, progressing from 15RM -> 12RM -> 10RM -> 8RM -> 6RM over 12 weeks
- 3x/week (NOT daily, unlike Alfredson)
- Full ROM: concentric + eccentric (3s each phase)
- Generally preferred for insertional tendinopathy where eccentric off step is contraindicated

**Insertional Tendinopathy Modification:**
- Perform eccentrics from FLAT GROUND only (no heel drop below neutral)
- Avoid dorsiflexion past 0 deg (compresses insertion against calcaneus)
- Use HSR protocol preferentially over Alfredson for insertional pathology

## Progression / Regression Criteria

### Progression Criteria

Advance to next phase when ALL met for 3 consecutive sessions:
1. Pain during exercise <= 3/10 (and not escalating during session)
2. No increase in swelling post-session (circumferential measurement stable or improving)
3. ROM improving or stable (DF within 2 deg of contralateral)
4. Weight-bearing lunge test improving (>1 cm improvement from baseline)
5. Form quality >= 80% across all exercises
6. RPE < 5/10 at current load
7. Single-leg balance: meets time threshold for current phase (see Balance Test Time Targets)
8. Gait: normalized pattern, no visible limp or compensatory strategy
9. FAAM score stable or improving

### Phase-Specific Advancement Criteria

| Transition | Key Criteria |
|---|---|
| Phase 1 -> Phase 2 | Pain-free walking on flat surfaces, swelling controlled, AROM >80% of normal |
| Phase 2 -> Phase 3 | Full ROM, single-leg balance >20s (eyes open), pain-free stairs, pain-free single-leg calf raise |
| Phase 3 -> Phase 4 | Single-leg balance >30s (eyes open), >20s (eyes closed), hop test >80% LSI, Y-balance composite >90% of contralateral |
| Phase 4 -> Discharge | Hop test battery >90% LSI, FAAM sport subscale >85%, sport-specific tasks completed without pain/apprehension |

### Regression Criteria

Regress to previous phase when ANY occur:
1. Pain during exercise > 5/10
2. Swelling increase post-session (>0.5 cm circumferential increase)
3. New or increased giving way / instability episodes
4. Form quality < 50% consistently despite cueing
5. FAAM worsening >= 8 points (exceeds MCID)
6. New pain location or pattern (reconsider diagnosis)
7. Night pain or rest pain increasing
8. Balance test time regressing >5 seconds from previous best

## Clinical Pearls

- **Ankle sprain recurrence:** 30-40% of lateral ankle sprains recur. Single greatest modifiable risk factor is proprioceptive deficit. 8-week balance training program reduces re-sprain risk by 50% (Hupperets et al. 2009). Every ankle sprain patient needs proprioceptive training regardless of severity.
- **Early mobilization > immobilization:** For Grade I-II lateral sprains, early functional treatment (brace + exercises) produces superior outcomes to immobilization in cast/boot. Weight-bear as tolerated from day 1 if Ottawa rules negative. Grade III may benefit from short (1-2 week) immobilization before mobilization.
- **Syndesmosis recovery timeline:** High ankle sprains take 2-3x longer to recover than lateral sprains. Athletes often return too early. Expected timeline: 6-16 weeks for sport return (vs. 2-6 weeks for lateral sprain). Incomplete syndesmosis healing leads to chronic ankle instability and early talocrural OA.
- **Achilles tendinopathy: HSR vs. Alfredson:** Both produce equivalent tendon pain and functional outcomes at 12 weeks and 1 year (Beyer et al. 2015). HSR has higher patient satisfaction (3x/week vs. daily) and better compliance. HSR preferred for insertional tendinopathy (no drop below neutral). Alfredson remains gold standard for midportion with strong evidence base.
- **Eccentric loading contraindication for insertional tendinopathy:** Heel drops off a step edge compress the Achilles insertion against the superior calcaneus and can worsen insertional symptoms. Use flat-surface eccentrics or HSR instead.
- **Plantar fasciitis: high-load strength training:** Rathjens et al. and Rathleff et al. (2015) showed high-load progressive strength training (single-leg calf raise with towel roll under toes = windlass activation) superior to stretching alone at 3 months. Protocol: 3x/week, 3 sets x 12 reps, progress to 10RM -> 8RM over 12 weeks.
- **Running gait analysis for chronic issues:** Chronic Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis, and recurrent ankle sprains warrant gait analysis. Key findings: excessive pronation (Achilles, PT tendinopathy), rearfoot striking (Achilles load), narrow step width (peroneal overload), crossover gait (IT band, ankle instability).
- **The foot-ankle-knee-hip chain:** Ankle dorsiflexion restriction (<35 deg WB lunge) forces compensations up the chain: early heel rise -> shortened stride -> increased knee flexion moment -> increased hip flexor demand. Restoring ankle DF can resolve proximal symptoms.
- **Y-balance test for functional assessment:** Anterior, posteromedial, posterolateral reach distances normalized to leg length. Composite score <89% of contralateral or anterior reach asymmetry >4 cm = increased injury risk. Use as objective return-to-sport criterion.
- **Bracing and taping for return to sport:** Semi-rigid brace or lace-up ankle brace reduces recurrence by 50-70% in first season after lateral ankle sprain. Taping effective but loosens after 20-30 minutes of activity. Recommend bracing for minimum 6-12 months post-sprain during sport.

### Functional Test Battery for Return-to-Sport Clearance

| Test | Target LSI | Method | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single-leg hop for distance | >90% | Max single-leg hop, measure distance, compare sides | Power, confidence |
| Triple crossover hop | >90% | 3 consecutive medial-lateral hops for distance | Lateral stability, power |
| Side hop test | >90% | Max lateral hops over 15 cm line in 30 seconds | Lateral ankle stability, endurance |
| Figure-8 hop test | >90% | Timed figure-8 course on one leg | Agility, dynamic stability |
| Y-balance test | Composite >94%, anterior asymmetry <4 cm | Reach in 3 directions on single leg | Dynamic balance, neuromuscular control |

**Reassessment schedule:** NPRS every session. FAAM (or VISA-A for Achilles) at baseline, 2 weeks, 6 weeks, 12 weeks, and discharge. Functional test battery at Phase 3 entry and at return-to-sport decision point.
